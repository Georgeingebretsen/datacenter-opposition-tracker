Three real fights

The pattern: organized, local, and tied to a specific stake.

Community won

Pittsylvania County, Virginia

vs. Balico · April 2025

3,500MWproposed
2,200acresplanned
84buildingsin the campus
Who organized: Save Our Farmlands Southern Environmental Law Center Appalachian Voices

Balico proposed a 2,200-acre campus with 84 data center buildings and its own 3,500 MW natural gas power plant — a project so large it would have rivaled a mid-sized state's entire generation capacity.

A coalition of farmland advocates, environmental lawyers, and regional conservation groups turned out to board meetings for months. In April 2025, the Board of Supervisors voted to deny the rezoning. The campus is dead.

The pattern: legal firepower + a specific local stake (farmland) + sustained attendance at the votes that mattered.

Community lost

Richland Parish, Louisiana

vs. Meta (Hyperion) · ongoing

2,200MWMeta's largest build ever
23Mgal/daygroundwater draw
$27Binvestment
Who organized: Earthjustice Sierra Club Union of Concerned Scientists Alliance for Affordable Energy

Hyperion is Meta's largest data center ever. It sits on a site that has grown from 2,250 to 3,650 acres as Meta quietly bought up adjacent land. To power it, Meta struck an Entergy deal for ten new gas-fired plants — 7.5 GW of new capacity, a 30%+ increase to Louisiana's entire grid.

Earthjustice filed. The Louisiana Public Service Commission declined to investigate a Blue Owl Capital financing arrangement that leaves ratepayers potentially exposed to infrastructure costs Meta will walk away from after a 4-year lease.

The pattern: state-level regulators and financing structures sit above the local levers that usually work. Opposition is well-organized but the fight is in the wrong venue.

Unresolved

Southaven, Mississippi

vs. xAI (Colossus 2) · active

27turbinesrunning unpermitted
$20B+planned investment
1,364sigson shutdown petition
Who organized: Safe and Sound Coalition NAACP Southern Environmental Law Center Earthjustice

xAI installed 27 gas turbines at the Colossus 2 site — capable of generating 495 MW, running without any air permit. Residents describe the noise as "like jet engines," day and night. Respiratory complaints and insomnia are widespread in the surrounding majority-Black neighborhoods.

The Mississippi Department of Environmental Quality granted an air permit in March 2026 despite 300+ opponents at the hearing and zero public supporters. A lawsuit is active. Operations continue.

The pattern: environmental justice plus the fact that the facility is already running. Communities can win cleanup orders, but the turbines are already there.

Why scale doesn't scare them off

You'd expect bigger projects to draw bigger opposition. They don't. What mobilizes people isn't the gigawatts — it's the creek, the school, the farmland.

Median petition signatures stay roughly flat across project sizes. A 50 MW build in a small county draws about as many signatures as a 2 GW hyperscale campus. Opposition runs on local stakes. Scale is the wrong axis.

Chapter 5

The limits

What the success story leaves out.

The most common tactic in the tracker is also

the least effective.

— fights have relied primarily on public comment — showing up at hearings, submitting letters to the record. Of those resolved, communities won 2%. Public comment matters as an input to other tactics. On its own, it's a near-certain loss.

Who's beating the opposition

Hyperscalers lose less often than developers do. Size does buy one thing — the ability to absorb and outlast.

One honest note

This is a tracker of contested projects. When a data center is announced, permitted, and built without a fight, it doesn't appear in the database. That means the 63% win rate describes fights that happened — not projects that got built. A complete scoreboard would need to include the silent approvals, and there are many.

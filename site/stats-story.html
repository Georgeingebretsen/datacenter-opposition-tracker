Chapter 2

What they're fighting about

Ten recurring concerns, ranked by how often they appear in the tracker.

Data center opposition has a recurring set of concerns. The same categories come up across red and blue counties, rural and suburban areas. The tracker tags every fight with one or more of these. Most common first.

  1. 1

    Zoning and land use

    — —

    Whether a project is allowed on a particular parcel under existing rules. This is the most directly actionable concern because local boards control zoning. Fights here usually involve conditional use permits, rezoning requests, or the creation of new industrial overlay districts.

  2. 2

    Community impact

    — —

    A broad category covering quality of life harms: light pollution, dust during construction, truck traffic on local roads, disruption to neighborhood character. It shows up when residents feel a project will make daily life worse in ways that do not fit the other categories cleanly.

  3. 3

    Grid and energy

    — —

    The project's draw is large enough to strain the regional grid. In several states, hyperscaler commitments have triggered new gas plants or long-distance transmission upgrades. The question is often whether that infrastructure gets paid for by the developer or by everyone else on the grid.

  4. 4

    Water

    — —

    Evaporative cooling systems and humidification can use millions of gallons per day, drawn from aquifers, rivers, or municipal water supplies. The concern intensifies in drought-prone regions like Arizona, Texas, and the Southwest, where future water availability is already contested.

  5. 5

    Environmental

    — —

    Wetlands, wildlife habitat, air quality, emissions. A catch all category for environmental harm beyond water specifically. Often appears alongside lawsuits when there is a federal or state law to invoke.

  6. 6

    Transparency

    — —

    Developers frequently operate through shell LLCs with generic names. Officials are sometimes asked to sign non-disclosure agreements before they can learn what is being built on which parcel. Residents have a hard time finding out who is behind a project and on what timeline.

  7. 7

    Ratepayer impact

    — —

    When a data center requires new generation or transmission, a question follows: does the company pay for it, or does the cost get spread to residential ratepayers? State public utility commissions decide this. Meta's Hyperion project in Louisiana is the most watched case.

  8. 8

    Noise

    — —

    Cooling fans and gas turbines run twenty four hours a day, often within a few hundred feet of homes. Residents report trouble sleeping and persistent background noise. Noise ordinances are enforceable at the local level, which helps explain the high win rate on these fights.

  9. 9

    Farmland

    — —

    Conversion of working agricultural land to industrial use, usually irreversible. Farmland protection resonates across partisan lines in rural counties and tends to surface in state-level debates about siting rules.

  10. 10

    Tax incentives

    — —

    Property tax abatements and payment-in-lieu-of-taxes agreements reduce what a project contributes to the jurisdiction it impacts. Abatements are usually granted early in the permitting process, which makes them hard to reverse afterward. This is the only category in the top ten with a sub-50 percent win rate.

The concerns with the highest win rates share a pattern. They are concrete. Zoning, noise, and farmland are things residents can point to on a map or measure in decibels. More diffuse concerns like tax structure and transparency come up often but are harder to convert into the narrow decisions local boards can actually act on.

How they win

Three fights

These three differ in outcome but share a few features: dedicated community organization, a specific concrete stake, and attention concentrated on the decision points that actually mattered.

Community won

Pittsylvania County, Virginia

vs. Balico · April 2025

3,500MWproposed
2,200acresplanned
84buildingsin the campus
Who organized: Save Our Farmlands Southern Environmental Law Center Appalachian Voices

Balico proposed a 2,200-acre campus with 84 data center buildings and its own 3,500 MW natural gas power plant, a project so large it would have rivaled a mid-sized state's entire generation capacity.

A coalition of farmland advocates, environmental lawyers, and regional conservation groups turned out to board meetings for months. In April 2025, the Board of Supervisors voted to deny the rezoning. The campus is dead.

What worked here: legal representation from SELC and Appalachian Voices, farmland as the concrete stake residents could organize around, and sustained presence at every Board of Supervisors meeting in the months leading up to the vote.

Community lost

Richland Parish, Louisiana

vs. Meta (Hyperion) · ongoing

2,200MWMeta's largest build ever
23Mgal/daygroundwater draw
$27Binvestment
Who organized: Earthjustice Sierra Club Union of Concerned Scientists Alliance for Affordable Energy

Hyperion is Meta's largest data center ever. It sits on a site that has grown from 2,250 to 3,650 acres as Meta quietly bought up adjacent land. To power it, Meta struck an Entergy deal for ten new gas-fired plants totaling 7.5 GW of new capacity, a 30%+ increase to Louisiana's entire grid.

Earthjustice filed. The Louisiana Public Service Commission declined to investigate a Blue Owl Capital financing arrangement that leaves ratepayers potentially exposed to infrastructure costs Meta will walk away from after a 4-year lease.

Why it's slipping away: the consequential decisions are happening at state regulators and in private capital markets. The air permit, the Entergy power purchase deal, and the Blue Owl financing all sit above the level where local opposition can reach. The groups organized against Hyperion are well resourced, but the levers that usually work in the tracker aren't available in this fight.

Southaven, Mississippi

vs. xAI (Colossus 2) · active

27turbinesrunning unpermitted
$20B+planned investment
1,364sigson shutdown petition
Who organized: Safe and Sound Coalition NAACP Southern Environmental Law Center Earthjustice

xAI installed 27 gas turbines at the Colossus 2 site, capable of generating 495 MW, running without any air permit. Residents describe the noise as "like jet engines," day and night. Respiratory complaints and insomnia are widespread in the surrounding majority-Black neighborhoods.

The Mississippi Department of Environmental Quality granted an air permit in March 2026 despite 300+ opponents at the hearing and zero public supporters. A lawsuit is active. Operations continue.

What makes this different: the environmental-justice framing brought in the NAACP and national legal groups, and the unpermitted operation gave the lawsuit a clear statutory hook. But the turbines were already running by the time MDEQ acted. Even a favorable ruling here means cleanup, not prevention.

Size doesn't predict opposition

You'd expect bigger projects to draw bigger opposition. The data doesn't show that.

Median petition signatures stay roughly flat across project sizes. A 50 MW build in a small county tends to draw about as many signatures as a 2 GW hyperscale campus. The variable that actually predicts opposition intensity is whether a project touches something concrete. A specific water source, a stretch of farmland, or a residential property line will mobilize residents. A large gigawatt figure by itself usually will not.

The limits

What the success story leaves out.

the least effective.

— community wins across — fights that used public comment, meaning showing up at hearings and submitting letters to the record. That works out to a — win rate among the resolved cases. Public comment appears in many winning fights, but usually alongside something else; when it's the only tool, it rarely decides the outcome.

Who's beating the opposition

Hyperscalers lose less often than smaller developers do. Size does buy one thing: the ability to absorb costs and outlast opposition.

Note

The tracker only records projects that drew some form of organized community opposition. When a data center is announced, permitted, and built quietly, as many are, it doesn't appear in the database. The 63% win rate describes the fights that happened, not the full universe of projects built in the U.S. over this period.
